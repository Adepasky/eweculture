The Ewe Language (Eʋegbe)

  • Home

Eʋegbe: Voice of a People

Ewe (Eʋegbe) is a Niger-Congo language spoken by approximately 6 million people in southeastern Ghana, southern Togo, and southwestern Benin. As a tonal language with a rich oral tradition, it serves as a vital vessel for preserving Ewe history, proverbs, and cultural identity.

Linguistic Features

Ewe is a tonal language with three level tones (high, mid, low) and two contour tones. It has a complex system of noun classes and verb serialization. The language uses a Latin-based alphabet with some additional characters like ƒ, ɖ, ɣ, and ŋ.

Writing System

Ewe uses a modified Latin alphabet developed by German missionaries in the 19th century. It includes 30 letters: 7 vowels and 23 consonants. The writing system was standardized in the 20th century and is used in education, literature, and media.

Dialects

The main dialects are Anlo, Tongu, Vedome, Agu, Fiaga, and Adangbe. While mutually intelligible, they have distinct phonetic and lexical differences. The Anlo dialect is considered the standard for Ghanaian Ewe.
