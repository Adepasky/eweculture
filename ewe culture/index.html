About the Ewe People

The Ewe people are an ethnic group primarily located in southeastern Ghana, southern Togo, and southwestern Benin. With a population of about 6 million, they are known for their rich cultural heritage, vibrant festivals, and unique language.

The Ewe migrated from present-day Nigeria and Benin between the 11th and 13th centuries, settling in their current locations. They established several kingdoms and developed sophisticated political systems, arts, and agricultural practices.

Ewe society is traditionally organized around clans and villages, with a strong emphasis on communal living and respect for elders. Their culture places high value on music, dance, and storytelling as means of preserving history and traditions.

Ewe Festivals

Hogbetsotso Festival

The most famous Ewe festival celebrated by the Anlo Ewe people in November. It commemorates their migration from Notsie in present-day Togo to their current homeland in Ghana.

Asafotu Festival

A warrior festival celebrated by the Ada people in August to commemorate victories in past wars and to honor ancestors. Features mock battles and military displays.

Yam Festival (Te Za)

Celebrated in September to thank the gods for a good harvest and to purify the people for the new year. Features feasting, dancing, and offerings of new yams.

Ewe Dances

Agbadza

The most popular Ewe dance, performed at social gatherings and funerals. It involves graceful movements and is accompanied by drums, gongs, and singing.

Borborbor

A high-energy dance from the central Ewe areas, characterized by fast footwork and hip movements. Often performed at celebrations and political rallies.

Ewe Language (Eʋegbe)

Ewe is a Niger-Congo language spoken by about 3 million people in Ghana and Togo. It's a tonal language with a rich oral tradition, proverbs, and storytelling culture.

Basic Ewe Phrases

Hello

Woé zɔ (Good morning)

Maɖo (Hello/Thank you)

How are you?

Ɛfɔ́a?

Response: Mɛfɔ́ nyuie (I'm fine)

Cultural Evolution

Pre-Colonial Era

Traditional Ewe society was organized around clans and villages with a strong oral tradition, indigenous religious practices, and subsistence farming.

Colonial Period (1884-1957)

German and later British/French rule introduced Western education and Christianity, leading to cultural changes while some traditions were preserved.

Post-Independence

After Ghana's independence in 1957, Ewe culture experienced a revival with renewed interest in traditional music, dance, and festivals.

Modern Era

Contemporary Ewe culture blends traditional elements with modern influences, seen in music genres like borborbor and agbadza fusion.

Tourism Sites in Ewe Land

Keta Lagoon Complex

A Ramsar wetland site important for biodiversity and birdwatching, with traditional fishing villages along its banks.

Tafi Atome Monkey Sanctuary

A sacred grove home to mona monkeys, protected by local traditions and now a community-based ecotourism site.

Wli Waterfalls

The highest waterfall in West Africa, located in the lush Agumatsa Wildlife Sanctuary near the Togo border.

Ewe Cuisine

Akple with Okro Soup

A staple dish made from corn dough served with a slimy okra soup, often with fish or meat.

Abolo

Steamed corn dough often eaten with pepper sauce or fried fish, popular along the coast.

Yeke Yeke

A fermented corn dish similar to kenkey but with a distinct preparation method and taste.
